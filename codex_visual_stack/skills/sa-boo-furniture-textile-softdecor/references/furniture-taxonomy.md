# Furniture Taxonomy and Object Intelligence

Use this as the first classification layer before style or sourcing.

## Primary categories

| Category | Subtypes | What to inspect |
|---|---|---|
| Seating | sofa, sectional, lounge chair, dining chair, stool, bench, ottoman, office chair | seat height/depth, back angle, arm height, cushion construction, upholstery seams, legs/base, clearance |
| Tables | dining, coffee, side, console, desk, nesting, pedestal | height relationship, top thickness, edge detail, leg/base stability, material weight, cable/wire needs |
| Storage/casegoods | cabinet, sideboard, wardrobe, bookshelf, nightstand, vanity | door/drawer clearance, hardware, plinth/leg, internal use, ventilation, anti-tip, finish durability |
| Bed/bedding | bed frame, headboard, mattress, bedding layers, bedside setup | mattress size, headboard height/thickness, nightstand relation, lighting, textile layering |
| Lighting/decor objects | floor/table lamp, pendant, sconce, mirror, art, vessel, plant, books, tray | scale, glare, focal height, wiring, reflection, styling hierarchy, maintenance |
| Outdoor/contract | lounge, dining, modular, parasol, acoustic furniture | weather resistance, UV, drainage, cleanability, stacking, code/performance |

## Dimension heuristics

Use project-specific standards when available. If exact standards are unavailable, mark numbers as approximate and verify against product data.

- Seating comfort depends on **seat height + depth + back angle + cushion firmness**, not just overall dimensions.
- Sofa seat depth should match posture: upright conversation needs shallower support; lounging needs deeper cushion and throw pillows.
- Dining chairs need enough pull-out space and leg clearance under the table.
- Coffee tables usually align near sofa seat height or slightly lower; leave comfortable walking clearance.
- Rugs should intentionally anchor furniture; avoid “floating postage stamp” rugs.
- Curtains need fullness, stack-back, track/pole clearance, and hem logic; textile amount changes the room more than a small decor object.

## Construction cues

### Upholstered seating

Inspect:

- frame material: hardwood, plywood, metal, composite;
- support: springs, webbing, platform, foam layers;
- cushion: high-resilience foam, down/feather wrap, fiberfill, loose vs fixed;
- seam/piping/tufting: craft signal and maintenance burden;
- legs/base: visual weight, cleaning clearance, robot vacuum clearance;
- removable covers: maintenance and replacement logic.

### Wood / casegoods

Inspect:

- solid wood vs veneer vs engineered board;
- edge banding and corner durability;
- joinery visibility and alignment;
- drawer runners/hinges/hardware quality;
- finish resistance to water, heat, scratching, sunlight;
- wall fixing / anti-tip where tall or child-accessible.

### Metal / stone / glass

Inspect:

- metal finish: plated, powder-coated, brushed, patinated; fingerprint and scratch risk;
- stone: slab thickness, edge profile, veining direction, sealing, weight support;
- glass: tempered/laminated, edge safety, reflection/glare, cleaning burden.

## Style-independent evaluation

A good object usually has:

1. a clear silhouette readable from a distance;
2. material transitions that look intentional;
3. a believable relationship between comfort and form;
4. no fake luxury detail added only for decoration;
5. correct scale relative to architecture and people;
6. a maintenance story matching the client lifestyle.

## Furniture as spatial grammar

- **Anchor**: sofa, dining table, bed, large rug.
- **Bridge**: coffee table, console, bench, side table.
- **Vertical accent**: floor lamp, screen, plant, shelving, art.
- **Tactile layer**: cushion, throw, curtain, bedding, rug.
- **Ritual object**: tea table, reading chair, writing desk, vanity, entry tray.

In SA&BOO work, furniture should not simply fill a plan. It should clarify ritual, hierarchy, memory, and movement.
