# Layout Optimization Logic Learned from SC071

Use this as the design reasoning layer after retrieving comparable source assets.

## Core mental model

Optimize a residential plan by coordinating three networks:

1. **Public/private network**: entry → living/dining/kitchen → semi-private study/family room → bedrooms/suites.
2. **Service/wet network**: kitchen, baths, laundry, housekeeping, storage, shafts, drainage, exhaust.
3. **Light/movement network**: daylight edges, ventilation, view axes, circulation width, door swings, furniture clearances.

The strongest schemes usually preserve technical cores, reduce corridor-only area, and convert circulation into useful storage, social, or flexible space.

## Step-by-step reasoning

1. **Freeze hard constraints**: structural walls/columns, shafts, exterior windows, façade, beams, plumbing stacks, gas/exhaust, entry door, balcony drains, code limitations.
2. **Define the brief**: actual residents, privacy level, hosting frequency, cooking style, WFH/study needs, elder/child/pet needs, storage intensity, future change.
3. **Map wasted area**: long corridors, oversized halls, dead corners, duplicate circulation, door-conflict zones, unusable wall fragments.
4. **Set anchors**:
   - Entry storage + buffer.
   - Kitchen/dining relation.
   - Main public zone and daylight edge.
   - Master or primary suite.
   - Wet-area cluster and laundry/housekeeping path.
5. **Generate 2-4 concepts**: keep the same technical feasibility, vary public-zone orientation, bedroom count, suite strategy, and flexible-room boundary.
6. **Score each concept**: function fit, daylight, privacy, storage, circulation, wet feasibility, furniture scale, construction risk, resale/adaptability.

## Common optimization moves

- **Compress corridors**: move doors, align openings, merge passage with dining/library/storage, avoid “corridor as leftover”.
- **Create a useful entry**: shoe/coat cabinet, delivery/cleaning buffer, visual screen, route to kitchen without crossing private bedrooms.
- **Integrate LD/K**: living-dining-kitchen can share daylight and enlarge perceived area, but control oil-smoke and appliance/storage depth.
- **Use +1 rooms flexibly**: study, guest room, kids activity, dressing, storage, or future bedroom; use sliding/transparent/half-open boundaries when daylight is weak.
- **Upgrade suites when area allows**: master bedroom + dressing + bath; keep wet adjacency and avoid sacrificing the only good public daylight.
- **Concentrate wet/service spaces**: kitchen/bath/laundry near shafts; avoid moving toilets far from stack; use dry/wet split when it improves morning peak usage.
- **Build storage as architecture**: entry wall, corridor wall, bed-wall, dining sideboard, laundry cabinet, under-stair, island/back-cabinet; storage must not pinch circulation.
- **Create loops only when useful**: 洄游 improves large-flat/villa flow, but in small units it can waste walls; loop width and destination must be real.
- **Correct irregular shells**: use cabinets, islands, benches, or angled partitions to absorb斜墙/异型 corners; keep furniture rectangles clear.

## Area-based patterns

### <50㎡ 一居 / compact studio

- Prioritize one strong public core over many weak rooms.
- Use bed privacy through partial wall, sliding screen, or raised/storage platform rather than full dark partition.
- Combine dining/work/storage where possible.
- Avoid: over-partition, fake two-bedroom, corridor bedrooms, undersized kitchen and bath created only to increase room count.

### 50-79㎡ one/two-bedroom

- Choose between “spacious one-bedroom + study” and “true two-bedroom”; do not force both if daylight/width fails.
- Keep living/dining near best light; use open or semi-open kitchen if cooking habits allow.
- Use entry + corridor storage to compensate for smaller bedrooms.
- Compare positives and反例 from 65/67/70/73㎡ source sets.

### 80-119㎡ 2+1 / 3-room

- Decide whether +1 is study, child room, guest room, or storage room; its boundary affects public-zone quality.
- Minimize bedroom-door clutter around the public zone.
- Maintain kitchen-dining efficiency and avoid cutting dining into circulation.
- Consider one dry/wet bath upgrade only if shafts and width allow.

### 120-159㎡ improvement flat

- Usually optimize around 3+1/4-room balance, entry storage, a stronger dining/kitchen relation, and primary-suite hierarchy.
- Avoid sacrificing all flexible area to bedroom count; keep a family/public core with scale.
- Use 135㎡3+1 and 153㎡3+1 source sets for positive/negative comparison.

### 160-219㎡ large flat

- Plan for family public zone + private suites + service storage.
- Create a clean guest route and a separate family/service route where possible.
- Add housekeeping/laundry/storage near kitchen/balcony/service side.
- Avoid oversized empty halls; convert them into library, family room, pantry, or loop anchor.

### 220㎡+ / 大平层 / duplex / villa

- Zone by layer or axis: guest/public, family/private, service/housekeeping, leisure.
- Treat stairs as a spatial and circulation anchor, not leftover.
- Use secondary kitchens, laundry rooms, storage rooms, equipment rooms, and staff/service paths when scale supports them.
- In villas, check vertical stacking, entry sequence, garage/service entry, elder room, guest privacy, and outdoor relation.

## Typology-specific patterns

### 平层

- Best optimization often comes from door relocation, corridor compression, LD/K reorientation, storage walls, and suite re-bundling.
- Preserve daylight for living and primary rooms.

### 异型平层

- Find the largest clean rectangle first.
- Put storage/cabinets/service along irregular edges.
- Avoid placing beds/sofas/dining tables directly against斜墙 unless dimensions work.

### 复式 / loft

- Separate noisy/public lower level from quiet/private upper level when possible.
- Check stair landing, under-stair storage, headroom, and bathroom stacking.
- Avoid forcing too many bedrooms upstairs if circulation becomes dark or narrow.

### 大平层

- Build hierarchy: entry ritual → public social core → family area → suite wing.
- Use loops, double dining/kitchen, and panoramic daylight only when they create real convenience.
- Avoid “hotel corridor” plans with many doors and no family core.

### 别墅

- Plan vertical logic first: public/reception, family bedrooms, guest/elder, leisure, service/equipment.
- Keep service circulation practical: groceries → kitchen/pantry; laundry → drying/storage; garage → mudroom; staff/service if needed.
- Avoid scenic but inefficient stairs that break privacy or create unusable leftover halls.

## Positive signals in source precedents

Look for these when opening retrieved assets:

- Public zone receives the best daylight and has clear furniture scale.
- Entry is buffered and useful, not just a door opening into living room clutter.
- Kitchen and dining are adjacent with storage/appliance logic.
- Bedroom doors do not dominate the living room view.
- Storage is continuous and deep enough.
- Wet spaces remain technically plausible.
- Flexible rooms borrow light and can change use.
- Large plans have hierarchy and service logic, not only more rooms.

## Negative / pitfall signals

Use反例 to guard against:

- Over-partitioning: many rooms but none comfortable.
- Long dark corridors and leftover halls.
- Door swing conflicts at entry, bath, bedroom cluster, and kitchen.
- Public area used as unavoidable traffic crossing.
- Kitchen isolated from dining or undersized for the stated cooking habit.
- Bath/toilet moved too far from drainage or exhaust.
- Bedroom created without daylight/privacy/usable furniture wall.
- Storage added in a way that narrows key circulation below practical use.
- Large flat/villa plans with impressive area but weak daily route logic.

## Evaluation rubric

Score each concept 1-5 on:

- Constraint feasibility.
- Functional fit to residents.
- Circulation clarity and width.
- Daylight/ventilation distribution.
- Public/private hierarchy.
- Kitchen/bath/service feasibility.
- Storage quantity and placement.
- Furniture scale and wall usability.
- Construction complexity/cost.
- Future adaptability.

Prefer the option with the best total fit, not the highest room count.
